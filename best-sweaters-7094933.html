The 19 Best Sweaters of 2024

Sweater weather has commenced, and we couldn’t be more excited to bundle up in our favorite knits to get this cozy season going. As a certified summer enthusiast, the only things that really thrill me about winter weather are one, mulled wine and two, the endless layering options of winter fashion. And an essential piece to layering effectively is the sweater. 

Because sweaters are a relatively broad category, we searched far and wide for the best options based on material, cut, and design. We've found stylish cardigans, toasty wool options, and ultra-soft cashmere pieces as well. We even tapped three fashion stylists for advice on how to style our favorite pieces. Michaela Leitz, a fashion stylist based in Frankfurt, ultimately recommends cashmere sweaters, calling them the go-to option in the winter. "Cashmere feels very soft, cozy, and breathable, and the fact that they are often thinner makes them perfect for packing during winter trips." Still, we included plenty of other options on this list so you can stock up your winter closet.

What We Don't Love: You have to get it dry cleaned or wash it by hand.

Typically, finding a cashmere sweater under the $100 mark is quite rare, but this one from Uniqlo meets us right at the mark. Not only is it impossibly soft, but the crewneck style is universally loved, easy to style, and always elegant. With all things cashmere, however, be extra careful with how you wash it. Uniqlo recommends hand washing cold or dry cleaning, and avoiding any hangers as it might stretch the item out.

What to Keep in Mind

Material

A sweater is supposed to be two things: comfortable and cute. In order to dive into the first point, let’s talk about the material. To keep it affordable, we found many pieces that contain polyester, acrylic, and nylon, which, yes, have a bad reputation. But when mixed with natural fibers, like wool, cashmere, and cotton, you’ll receive the same benefits, only at a lower price. Natural fibers tend to be soft, water-resistant, and durable, which is why they’re our go-to pick for sweater material. 

Wool, which can be found in the Henne Dario Knit is a highly breathable material that simultaneously has excellent temperature regulation properties, making it ideal for very cold days. Although wool is known at times to be itchy, it still makes a great layering piece. “I make sure to layer a cotton T-shirt underneath since I have sensitive skin,” says Theodora. Leitz also recommends buying a wool sweater with some cashmere mixed in for a softer feel as well as washing the sweater to eliminate the scratchy feel. A great example of this can be found in the Recreational Habits Athos Cropped Turtleneck Sweater.

Cashmere, on the other hand, is famous for its extraordinarily soft fibers. The Everlane The Cashmere Crew and Reformation Garret Cashmere Oversized Turtleneck will provide you with a luxurious look and feel. 

For a natural, non-animal byproduct, opt for cotton, which can be found in the majority of comfortable sweaters. There’s an endless amount of ways you can weave the material to create a worthwhile affordable option that provides both warmth and durability. “Sometimes we tend to sweat during winter beneath our thick coats, and cotton is known for its absorbing features,” says Leitz.

Fit

There is no right or wrong fit for a sweater — it’s all about what you feel most comfortable in — however, you should think about when you plan to wear it. For a casual everyday piece, go for a crewneck, like the Everlane The Cashmere Crew, that can be worn to just about any occasion. The oversized fit of the Dissh Dakota Cream Boucle Jumper makes more of a statement, but for you to have a little room to layer more pieces on top because it adds a lot of extra bulk. And of course, we cannot forget the turtleneck. Just like the Abercrombie Tuckable Easy Turtleneck Sweater, turtlenecks offer style, warmth, and versatility, but try to save these for truly chilly days because they’ll be the warmest choice. 

Your Questions, Answered

What is the best way to store sweaters?

Don’t hang sweaters — fold them, especially a wool or cashmere sweater, which is more prone to stretching when hung — and store them away in your dresser. Keep them in a cool, dry place, especially if stored for a long period of time. We even suggest investing in items like cedar blocks to store them in order to naturally keep moths away (and prevent your clothes from being chewed apart). 

What’s the best way to wash a sweater?

Depending on the material the sweater is made of, it may come with a few complicated washing instructions. Nobody likes to have their favorite sweater shrink in the dryer, or worse, pill and lose its soft texture. For this reason, you’ll want to make sure you follow the label’s instructions on how to wash your sweater before mindlessly tossing it into the machine with the rest of your clothes.

Cashmere is notoriously known for its disdain of washers and dryers — make sure to bring it to the dry cleaners in order to maintain its pristine condition. You’ll also want to hand wash anything that contains acrylic, as this fabric is susceptible to shrinkage in high heat. Meanwhile, wool items can typically be machine washed, so long as you are using a cold or room-temperature setting. As a matter of fact, it can help to take out some of the itchiness that’s associated with this material.

How would you style a sweater?

Of course, this answer depends on what style of sweater you choose. “I love styling my turtleneck sweaters with big silver jewelry, micro miniskirts, black tights, and fun chunky boots,” says Julia Fox’s stylist Briana Andalore. When it comes to oversized sweaters, Leitz likes to “style an oversized fluffy sweater with jeans, Chelsea boots, a big woolen scarf in a beautiful popping color like purple.” While balancing out a loose-fitted top with a form-hugging bottom is always a safe go-to, I personally love to go for a top-to-bottom baggy attire. Oversized on top of oversized can get sloppy quickly, so pairing it with a nice blazer or polished shoes is vital.
