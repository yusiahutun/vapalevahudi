Dan Levy's 'Good Grief' and why we need grief storylines on TV

Despite it affecting us all at some point in our lives, death and grief remain hard to talk about. Over the last few years, we’ve seen more grieving on a global scale than ever before during our lifetimes. And, although conversations are no doubt improving, death still feels like a taboo topic – making grief storylines and representation on our screens so important.

For others who also find themselves in the club no one wants to be in, you might, like me, find great comfort in turning to film and TV to watch grief portrayed in different ways (and the roller coaster of emotions that come with it). Daniel Levy’s Good Grief – which comes to Netflix on 5 January and marks Levy’s feature film directorial debut – tells the emotional story of Marc (played by Levy) as he navigates life as a widower after the death of his husband, Oliver (Luke Evans). Marc slowly rebuilds his shattered heart with the help of his two closest friends, Thomas and Sophie (Himesh Patel and Ruth Negga), during a soul-searching trip to Paris.

Just watching the trailer had me welling up. “I’ve been reading that the brain is like a muscle – that’s why getting over death is so hard, because your brain has been trained to feel things for a person,” says Levy, as a montage of Marc and his late husband plays. “When they go away, your head is still operating under the impression that it should feel those things for that person, like muscle memory.” This encapsulates the feeling of grief perfectly: your mind and heart long to be with that person again, but instead, you're left with an ache that takes years to heal.

Levy, who rose to fame for his sensitive, nuanced performance as David Rose on Schitt’s Creek, found inspiration for Good Grief in the passing of his beloved dog, Redmond, and the death of his grandmother. “I think it was a lack of clarity on my emotional journey that led me to Good Grief, to be honest,” Levy explained to Forbes. “I didn’t know what to make of my brain, I didn’t know what to make of my heart, I didn’t know what to make of the feelings that I was experiencing.”

Dan Levy in Good Grief, which comes to Netflix in January 2024

Good Grief reminds me of the famous quote by the author Jamie Anderson: “Grief, I’ve learned, is really just love. It’s all the love you want to give, but cannot. All that unspent love gathers up in the corners of your eyes, the lump in your throat, and in that hollow part of your chest. Grief is just love with no place to go.” Levy's project wouldn’t be as sensitive and as emotionally accurate if he hadn’t experienced not only the pain that comes with losing someone, but also the beauty in learning to laugh and love again, with the help of those friends that stick by your side no matter how ugly things get.

“Grief is deeply isolating and can cause feelings of loneliness, which others can find difficult to understand,” explains TV psychologist Emma Kenny. “When grief is portrayed and represented on TV and social media it reminds the individual struggling that they’re not alone and, more importantly, that they are acknowledged and understood. Grief can be traumatising and complex, so when they see others saying that these reactions are understandable and also common, it can help soothe the sting of loss.”

Loss is a terrifying, isolating thing that seems a foreign language to those yet to go through it, so watching a character's experiences onscreen can help someone to understand what they don't yet know. Likewise, for those who are grieving, it's comforting to feel that you aren't alone. But, most importantly, these stories provide spaces to talk about death and grief more openly.

I've found great comfort in shows like This Is Us, After Life, Euphoria and the Sex and the City reboot And Just Like That.... The Pearson family in This Is Us learn how to keep someone's memory alive, as well as the importance of letting go and creating new memories entirely. When Rebecca (played beautifully by Mandy Moore) returns home to tell her children that their father, Jack (Milo Ventimiglia) has died, I cried uncontrollably; I remember that day in my own story painfully well. In fact, I lost count of how many times I cried with my partner on the sofa watching all six seasons.

This Is Us fast became my favourite show I've ever watched: I felt like I had space to grieve again and process those lost emotions that time rubs out. Ricky Gervais’ After Life continued to depict how anger can take over when you lose someone you love; Zendaya’s lead in Euphoria as troubled teenager Rue demonstrated the bad decisions we might make to numb the pain of loss; while the return of Carrie Bradshaw highlighted the heartbreaking reality of being a widow.

“Sadly, we don’t talk nearly enough about death and dying, and because we fail to normalise this completely natural human event, we make the process of grieving feel like a secret emotional experience which can feel very abandoning,” notes Kenny. “To see others reflect your feelings, sentiments, process and pain openly creates a permission base for you to do the same.”

When And Just Like That... first landed on our screens in 2021, I watched Big’s funeral through bleary eyes, my best friend and I clinging to each other. We knew exactly which moment we'd been transported back to – saying goodbye to my dad, who died in 2014. He was as much of a father figure to her as he was to me, regularly teasing her for her ginger hair or doing a special bedtime show with our teddies. But most of all, he loved how much we loved each other. It breaks my heart that he never knew we finally played out our Mary-Kate and Ashley childhood games in our 'New York apartment', instead swapping the East Village for our East London flat. Or that every time we do karaoke, we sing our hearts out to Anastasia, because she was his guilty pleasure. Watching the scene together almost gave us permission to talk about him and our shared grief.

“By opening up these conversations, an opportunity for emotional catharsis is presented,” explains Kenny. “The more grief is normalised, the more those grieving feel entitled to speak about their pain and loss.” It’s true – this moment made me feel less alone. When we grieve, it’s easy to not make the time for it, but this moment made us do just that.

“How long it seems that we’ve known him and yet, not near long enough.” – Miranda Hobbs, And Just Like That...

I’m not the only one finding comfort on screen. “Never Have I Ever made me realise that grief comes in such different ways and you have to do what’s best for you,” writer Michelle Theil tells me. She lost her dad aged 19 and says she found great comfort in the Netflix show, which also helped her decide to go to therapy. “The show has a character who lost her dad and she kind of carried on with things like I did, and for a long time I continued to still feel like a little kid.”

ISABELLA B. VOSMIKOVA/NETFLIX

Never Have I Ever is another show credited with showing grief in a helpful way

“I felt guilty that I wasn’t grieving in the same way my mum was, but the show made me realise it’s such a personal journey and everyone does it differently. You don’t have to do what people are telling you to do, it takes time to realise what’s best for you.”

In the final episodes of Euphoria's second season, Rue opens up to Lexi (Maude Apatow) about how she’s dealt with the loss of her father by numbing herself with drug abuse. She has watched her own journey re-enacted in the form of Lexi’s school play, and seen how her behaviour looked through the eyes of her former best friend. We watch as the play showcases Rue’s father’s funeral over and over again; how Rue spirals and repeatedly turns to drugs. She watches her life change on stage, but the performance also gives her a new perspective on her trauma. She decides from that moment to be more forgiving of herself.

“We learn things from other people, from TV and film,” agrees Theil. “We internalise a lot, so if we’re watching a show with grief portrayal and you have a scene where the family and friends are supporting and talking to each other, then you file that away in the back of your mind and apply it to real-life when it happens to someone close to you.”

“I miss you, Dad. I miss you until I close my eyes.” – Rue Bennett, Euphoria

More often than not, we fear making a sad situation even worse by saying the wrong thing; however, our on-screen friends can help with that too. “If we watch someone say something they shouldn’t have said to someone who is grieving, we apply that too,” adds Theil. “It allows for people to be more open and not feel scared to talk about it – it's really damaging to suffer in silence.”

“There is no perfect way to support anyone through grief as we are all built emotionally differently,” advises Kenny. “The main thing to remember is that it’s better to say or do something 'wrong', than to say or do nothing at all. It’s astonishing how many friends disappear when someone is facing a critical loss, and inevitably this kind of abandonment can add to an already hyper-painful time. The best action is to just show up and ask them what they’d find helpful.”

Crucially, television reminds us that there is no right or wrong way to deal with grief, but that we should give ourselves the permission to discuss our feelings openly. However, watching grief representation onscreen when you’re going through it yourself can be incredibly difficult and triggering. Do so sensitively, research a show's content beforehand, and make sure you're in the right frame of mind before jumping in.

Here’s hoping for more sensitively told stories.

What do I do if I get triggered by grief onscreen?

“Whenever grief strikes out of the blue, and you’re wondering why you’re suddenly overwhelmed, take note of what’s happening to make you feel that way,” advises Kenny. “Usually, it’s something that may have caused you to remember an obscure detail of your loved one, maybe a scene in a hospital, or someone talking about a death that reflects the one you experienced. Try to reflect on what aspect affected you most as this will offer insight as to why you responded so strongly.

"You cannot avoid triggers permanently, but you can choose to take time away from programmes and social media feeds that cover the aspect you have identified. Remember that part of the healing process is to gradually feel less devastated and therefore less regularly triggered and to notice this improvement you will need to expose yourself to certain difficult reminders over time as these will help you gauge where you are on your healing journey.”

How to help those grieving

“You cannot heal the grief, but you are likely able to help them keep on top of the cleaning or paying bills – and you can ensure they have enough nutritious food in their fridge ,as people often stop practising self-care when they are struggling,” says Kenny. “Allow them to cry as they need and just be present with them when they do, avoid telling them that things will be ok, or that time will heal their loss and instead, just reassure them that you intend to support them during this awful time. Allow them to talk about their loved one and don’t be afraid of sharing your own stories and recollections. This person may be lost, but they will definitely not be forgotten.”
