Catching up with designer Tamara Ralph

It’s rare for a fashion label to make a splash before the designer has even presented her debut collection. But in the weeks leading up to the Paris couture shows in July, everyone from Halle Berry to Kate Hudson turned to Tamara Ralph for a gala gown. Queen Rania of Jordan wore a sorbet-yellow dress by the Australian couturière to King Charles’ coronation. Lily James channelled Odette/Odile in Swan Lake by appearing first in snowy, pearl-encrusted silk at the Baftas and then in wicked black leather at the Met Ball – both straight from Ralph’s London atelier.

Was the designer surprised by the A-list clamour for her creations? Not in the slightest. "I think they were just saying, 'Thank gosh you’re back!'," she says with a smile. While Tamara Ralph Couture is brand-new, she has been supplying the world’s richest and most beautiful women with fairy-tale gowns for almost 20 years.

Ralph’s couture atelier is in Millbank, near the Thames in London, but she lives with her partner Bhanu Choudhrie, an Indian-born billionaire, and their two-year-old daughter, Haliya, in Monaco. The couple met at an event in Bordeaux, organised by the Young Presidents’ Organisation, which arranges networking opportunities for top executives and entrepreneurs. They aren’t married yet, partly because Ralph would have to design her own wedding dress and she’s too busy with her new label’s first couture show to dream up a bridal extravaganza for herself.

The nature of her work means she spends her time between Monte Carlo and London, and Haliya travels with her. Today, however, Ralph is drinking tea in a central-London hotel, wearing an impeccable draped cream crêpe de chine on top of her own design, and a pair of matching Valentino trousers, her fiancé and daughter ensconced in a suite upstairs. It all seems too good to be true, but the dramas and difficulties of the past few years have taken a toll on Ralph. "If it wasn’t for my baby," she says, "I’m not sure I would have found the strength to begin again."

Behind today’s success lies a cautionary tale. In 2017, Ralph was riding high as one half of the couture house Ralph & Russo with her then partner, Michael Russo. They had been named in Fortune’s annual list of the 40 most successful people under 40 – the only representatives of the fashion world to get the nod. Theirs was the sole British house that the extremely picky Chambre Syndicale de la Haute Couture allowed to show as part of the official schedule during couture fashion weeks. And then the label became a household name when Meghan Markle chose a Ralph & Russo look for her official engagement pictures.

That dress created quite a rumpus – it was the wrong colour! It had a see-through top! It cost $75,000! – and Ralph says now that she thought "it was an interesting choice. It was quite a statement gown because it was black and potentially revealing and quite progressive". Whatever traditionalists might have made of it, the design was a social-media hit.

And then the fantasy began to unravel: the couple broke up and shortly afterwards, the pandemic hit and the bottom fell out of the couture market. Worse was to follow when the company went into administration amid claims of excessive spending, huge debts and alleged mismanagement of the business.

Ralph, who by this point was pregnant with her daughter, was devastated. She still finds talking about the bad times a challenge – when the subject comes up, her lip trembles and her sunny expression darkens. "I put my heart and soul into it," she says. "And what happened with the brand was incredibly traumatic. It was so heartbreaking for me – and what made it harder is that it was all happening at the same time I was giving birth."

She was trying to enjoy "the happiest moment of my life" while also going through "the most painful experience". Not surprising, perhaps, that she suffered from postnatal depression. "Being a new mother is hard enough, and having all that [stress about the business]… it impacted me a lot," she admits. "Women struggle even if they don’t have such a difficult situation. I wasn’t prepared for the emotions."

What Ralph found the most difficult was worrying about what would happen to her team of seamstresses and craftspeople. "I had worked with these people for most of my career," she explains. "And I considered them to be my family." It surely speaks for itself that everyone she recruited for her new label, Tamara Ralph Couture, was once a Ralph & Russo employee. "They believe in me," she says. "They believe in my creativity and we’re all very happy to be working together again.’

Not that Ralph thought she would be back at the couture shows a mere two years after her business imploded. "I wanted to take some time to enjoy my baby," she says. But her regular customers wouldn’t hear of it. "I had clients calling me up, saying 'Darling, I’ve nothing to wear.'" Another rang the new mother to say, "You’ve had your baby, so let’s start creating again".

Sooner or later, though, Ralph was always going to dive back into the world of fashion. It has been part of her life since her earliest childhood, and she is the fourth generation of her family to be in the design business. Her great-grandmother was a dressmaker, her grandmother was a couturière and her mother studied fashion before becoming an interior designer (she also made all of Ralph’s clothes herself). As a child, Ralph was fascinated by the sewing room of their house in Sydney. She would wait until her mother had left the house "and then I would take down her dress patterns and cut them up and create something new, which would upset her greatly," she remembers. "I started when I was around eight or nine, and when I was a young teenager I was allowed near the sewing machine." Ralph would take her designs to her grandmother at weekends and the older woman would critique them. "She would rip them apart and look inside and tell me everything that was wrong." There’s probably no better way to learn about craftsmanship and perfectionism.

By the time she was 12, Ralph was wearing her own outfit to the graduation ceremony from her primary school – black and covered with appliqué stars that she had stitched on by hand. Later, as a student at the Whitehouse Institute of Design, Australia’s equivalent of Central Saint Martins, she was always interested in the more expensive and exclusive end of fashion. Despite her tutors’ attempts to teach Ralph the benefits of utilitarian design, she never compromised and stuck to her own, glamorous, vision.

But Sydney in the early 2000s was "a much more casual society," recalls Ralph. "There weren’t many luxury stores at all." With no home market for her couture ambitions, she knew she had to move to Europe to do what she loved. So, aged 23, she found herself in London, where she had a stylist friend who took her under her wing. "I came over with a dream and a hope. It was a humble beginning but I worked really hard."

"If it wasn’t for my baby, I’m not sure I would have found the strength to begin again"

Essentially, Ralph’s entrepreneurial career began with her wearing her own designs to events and attracting the attention of other guests who would go on to become clients and, eventually, friends. One early customer, attracted by her black double-satin slinky evening gown embellished with hand-embroidered crystals, ordered six pieces from her on the spot and word spread. In 2006, she registered her company with Russo, having met the Australian banker on a street corner in London and fallen into love and a business partnership with him. (This used to be known as "fashion’s favourite fairy tale" before the dream shattered.) In 2014, she was at an event in Baku with her friend and client Zaha Hadid, when she was called and invited to show on schedule during Paris Couture.

These days, most of her customers are from the Middle East and Europe, though Chinese and Indian socialites are also queueing up for dresses that take up to six months and untold thousands to make. Many of them have been customers for years. "The thing about what I do," explains Ralph, "is that you’re involved in a lot of special moments. It could be a wedding, and that’s a happy and emotional time. Or it could be a suit someone wears to receive a damehood." (As it was in the case of Angelina Jolie.)

Angelina Jolie wearing Ralph & Russo to receive her damehood in 2014

Ralph’s special skill, she believes, is that "when I meet someone, I know exactly what they should wear. Designing bespoke is about enhancing everything they would want to enhance. That’s the beauty of couture." There’s also the matter of concealing anything they might want to hide, which is why couture appointments can be "very intimate. And you end up building a close bond." The slow-fashion couture process means she will meet a client, speak to them "and get a feel for who they are". She could be called upon to provide not just one special outfit, but a whole wardrobe of clothes that will last a customer through an entire season of dinners, yacht cruises, balls and parties. Jolie was a particular inspiration, says Russo. "I did quite a lot of work for her throughout the years – I enjoy working with her because she’s creative herself and she loves the whole process. She knows herself really well and she feeds in."

It’s surely only a matter of time before Jolie strikes a pose in a Tamara Ralph creation. But she will see a difference, for Ralph believes her designs have changed since she launched her new label. "I have always been a designer who celebrates femininity," she explains. "But I’ve grown stronger through my experiences, and I think that shows in the clothing. There’s a strength behind the pieces now – they almost have an armour built in. A lot of beautiful metalwork." She adds that her new designs are "a little bit more modern, grown-up and slightly cleaner".

"I’ve grown stronger through my experiences, and I think that shows in the clothing"

The maison itself is also stronger and simpler. "We’re all much wiser," she says of herself and the staff with whom she is now reunited. "The team is smaller and I’ve learnt lessons about how to structure things differently".

The label’s first couture show, held in July in the gilt-and-white Grand Salon of Paris’ Shangri-La hotel, demonstrated a different kind of structure. Of course, there were silken roses and mint-green ostrich-feather capes, but there were also pearl-encrusted wire corsets, metallic floral breastplates and a dress made of chainmail. It was a display of Ralph’s characteristic femininity and also a testament to her new strength. Haliya was backstage all day and, when her mother emerged at the end, she brought the child onto the catwalk with her to take her bow as well. "It’s only fair," Ralph tells me afterwards. "She really inspired me. You want your children to be proud of you and that’s my motivation". A pause, and a smile. "After all, I couldn’t have done it without her."
